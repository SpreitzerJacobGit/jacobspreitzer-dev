The version of me that existed before AI tools was limited by ==scope==. Not ability — scope. Projects that were too big to justify, problems that were too broad to start, domains too unfamiliar to enter alone. That ceiling is gone.

The clearest proof is a change tracking application I built for my team at work. Before, our development work lived in scattered systems with no single source of truth. I built a full application from scratch that normalizes our legacy data, connects to our modern ticketing pipeline, and includes a Claude integration so anyone on the team — technical or not — can ask plain-English questions about project status and workload. It ==wouldn't have been attempted== without AI. The scope alone would have killed it.

Outside of work, AI has become the thing I reach for when I want to get better at something. I use it to surface patterns in my health data I wouldn't have noticed on my own. I've built structured training frameworks for improving at table tennis and pickleball, gone deep into pu'erh fermentation and gongfu brewing technique, and planned cycling loads with more precision than I'd have designed alone. These aren't things I'd pursue ==systematically== before. Now getting better at what I actually care about is just a conversation away.

The judgment is still mine. What to build, what tradeoffs to make, what actually matters to a real person — AI doesn't decide any of that. What it does is remove the ==distance== between an idea and finding out if it works. That distance used to cost a lot.
